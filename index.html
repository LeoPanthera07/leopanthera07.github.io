About

I started in biological data science, where I fell for a single idea: that complex systems become knowable through data. Genomes, proteins, clinical signals — noise until you find the structure underneath. That pursuit led me, almost inevitably, into AI and machine learning.

For years I've worked at the intersection of biology, data, and software — building tools adopted by scientists around the world. Today I engineer AI systems: RAG pipelines, multi-agent architectures, and the LLMOps infrastructure that keeps them correct, observable, and shippable.

Outside the terminal: racing, philosophy, and personal finance — the same curiosity pointed at systems that aren't made of code. Because navigating complex systems was never only about software.

The unfair advantage

Biology was the hard part.

Most engineers building AI have never had to model data this unforgiving. Biological data is sparse, noisy, and high-dimensional, and it rarely comes with clean ground truth — a protein doesn't ship with labels, a genome doesn't fit in a spreadsheet, and a clinical signal is mostly noise. Years in biological data science were years spent learning to find the signal anyway.

That's the edge I carry into AI. I don't just wire up pipelines — I understand the domains where models quietly break. Bringing RAG, agents, and LLMOps to biomedical and scientific data isn't a pivot away from my background. It's the payoff for it: a decade of domain fluency that most AI engineers will never have, applied to the problems that need it most.

Hard data by default
